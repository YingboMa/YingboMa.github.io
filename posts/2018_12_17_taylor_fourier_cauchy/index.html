Taylor Series, Fourier Series, and Cauchy's Integral Formula

Dec 17, 2018

The shortest and the best way between two truths of the real domain often passes through the imaginary one.

–- Paul Painlevé

A Taylor series is a linear combination of monomials

f(z)=∑i=1ncizi. f(z) = \sum_{i=1}^n c_{i} z^i. f(z)=i=1∑n​ci​zi.

A Fourier series is a linear combination of sinusoidal functions

g(θ)=∑i=1ncieiθn. g(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n c_{i} e^{i \theta n}. g(θ)=i=1∑n​ci​eiθn.

Note that if we restrict the complex variable zzz to the unit circle eiθe^{i\theta}eiθ, we see a Fourier series in disguise. Namely, we have f(θ)=g(eiθ)f(\theta) = g(e^{i\theta})f(θ)=g(eiθ). Hence, real Taylor and Fourier series are special cases of a complex Taylor series.

The coefficients of a Taylor series can be obtained by differentiating the function fff at a point aaa:

cn=f(n)(a)n!. c_{n} = \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}. cn​=n!f(n)(a)​.

The coefficients of a Fourier series are obtained by integrating ggg with a sinusoidal wave that oscillates nnn times:

cn=12π∫02πg(θ)e−iθn dθ. c_{n} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} g(\theta) e^{-i\theta n}\; d\theta. cn​=2π1​∫02π​g(θ)e−iθndθ.

They must be equal, if we take zzz to be on the unit circle. We now have:

f(n)(a)n!=12π∫02πg(θ)e−iθn dθ. \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} g(\theta) e^{-i\theta n}\; d\theta. n!f(n)(a)​=2π1​∫02π​g(θ)e−iθndθ.

Remarkably, this equation says that we can use integration to compute high order derivatives of an analytical function.

We can rewrite the above equation's right-hand side in the form of a complex path integral. To be precise, the contour is a circle which centered at a∈Ca \in \mathbb{C}a∈C with radius 111.

Interestingly, we have Cauchy's integral formula:

f(n)(a)=n!2πi∮f(z)(z−a)n+1 dz. f^{(n)}(a) = \frac{n!}{2\pi i} \oint \frac{f(z)}{(z-a)^{n+1}}\; dz. f(n)(a)=2πin!​∮(z−a)n+1f(z)​dz.

Oh, what a coincidence!?
